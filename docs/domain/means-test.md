# Chapter 7 Bankruptcy Means Test Reference

> **Governing Forms:** Official Form 122A-1, 122A-1Supp, 122A-2
> **Statutory Basis:** 11 U.S.C. Section 707(b)(2), as enacted by BAPCPA (2005)
> **Last Reviewed:** April 2026

---

## 1. Purpose and Background

The Chapter 7 means test is a statutory screening mechanism introduced by the **Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005 (BAPCPA)** to prevent debtors with sufficient income from discharging debts under Chapter 7 when they could instead repay creditors through a Chapter 13 plan.

The means test determines whether granting Chapter 7 relief would constitute a **"presumption of abuse"** under 11 U.S.C. Section 707(b)(2). If the presumption arises, the U.S. Trustee, creditors, or other parties in interest may seek dismissal of the case or conversion to Chapter 13.

**Key principle:** The means test applies only to debtors with **primarily consumer debts** (more than 50% of total debt is personal/household/family). Debtors with primarily business debts are exempt.

---

## 2. The Two-Part Structure

### Part 1: Income Comparison to State Median (Form 122A-1)

The debtor calculates their **Current Monthly Income (CMI)** and annualizes it. This annualized figure is compared to the **median family income** for a household of the same size in the debtor's state.

- **If income is at or below the state median:** No presumption of abuse. The debtor passes the means test. Form 122A-2 is NOT required.
- **If income is above the state median:** The debtor must proceed to Part 2 (Form 122A-2) for detailed expense analysis.

### Part 2: Detailed Expense Deduction Calculation (Form 122A-2)

For above-median debtors, disposable income is calculated by subtracting allowed deductions from adjusted CMI. The resulting 60-month disposable income figure determines whether there is a presumption of abuse.

### Exemption from the Means Test (Form 122A-1Supp)

Certain debtors are entirely exempt from the means test:

1. **Non-consumer debtors:** Debts are primarily (more than 50%) non-consumer (business) debts
2. **Qualifying military service:** Debtor is a disabled veteran whose indebtedness occurred primarily during active duty or homeland defense activity, OR the debtor (whether or not a reservist) was on active duty or performing homeland defense activity for at least 90 days within 540 days before filing

If exempt, the debtor files Form 122A-1Supp and does not need to complete Form 122A-1 (beyond verification) or 122A-2.

## 3. Current Monthly Income (CMI) -- 11 U.S.C. Section 101(10A)

### Definition

CMI is the debtor's **average monthly gross income from all sources** received during the **6 full calendar months immediately preceding the bankruptcy filing date**.

### The 6-Month Lookback Period

The period ends on the **last day of the calendar month before the filing month**. For example:
- Filing date: September 15 --> lookback period: March 1 through August 31
- Filing date: January 3 --> lookback period: July 1 through December 31

The total income for all 6 months is added together and divided by 6.

### Income Sources INCLUDED in CMI

| Source | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| Gross wages, salary, tips, bonuses, overtime, commissions | Before all payroll deductions (Line 2) |
| Alimony and maintenance payments received | Not from a co-filing spouse (Line 3) |
| Regular contributions for household expenses | From unmarried partner, household members, dependents, parents, roommates. From spouse only if Column B is not filled in (Line 4) |
| Child support received | Included as household contribution (Line 4) |
| Net business income | Gross receipts minus ordinary and necessary operating expenses (Line 5) |
| Net rental/real property income | Gross receipts minus ordinary and necessary operating expenses (Line 6) |
| Interest, dividends, royalties | (Line 7) |
| Unemployment compensation | Unless it qualifies as a Social Security Act benefit (Line 8) |
| Pension/retirement income | Subject to exclusions below (Line 9) |
| All other income | Any source not listed above (Line 10) |

**Spousal income:** If married, the non-filing spouse's income is included in Column B of Form 122A-1 when the couple lives together (unless legally separated). However, any portion of the spouse's income NOT regularly used for household expenses of the debtor or dependents can be subtracted on Line 3 of Form 122A-2.

### Income Sources EXCLUDED from CMI

| Exclusion | Statutory Basis |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Social Security benefits (retirement, disability, SSI, survivor) | Social Security Act benefits excluded per 11 U.S.C. Section 101(10A)(B) |
| Payments to victims of war crimes, crimes against humanity, or terrorism | 11 U.S.C. Section 101(10A)(B) |
| VA disability compensation, combat-related injury/disability payments | Payments by U.S. Government in connection with disability, combat-related injury, or death of uniformed services member |
| Certain military retired pay | Chapter 61 (disability) retired pay is excluded except to the extent it exceeds what would have been received under non-disability retirement |

**Important:** Social Security income is reported separately on the form (not in the CMI calculation lines) so the court can see it, but it is NOT counted toward CMI.

## 4. State Median Income Thresholds

### Source and Updates

Median family income data comes from the **U.S. Census Bureau's American Community Survey (ACS)**. The **U.S. Trustee Program** (Department of Justice) publishes the applicable figures, typically updating them **twice per year** (around April/May and November).

The applicable median is determined by:
1. **State of residence** (where the debtor lives at filing)
2. **Household size** (number of people in the household)

### Household Size Determination

The Bankruptcy Code does **not formally define "household size."** Courts have adopted three approaches:

1. **Census Bureau / "heads on beds":** Everyone living under the same roof, regardless of relationship or financial contribution
2. **Income tax dependents:** The number of exemptions the debtor could claim on their federal tax return
3. **Economic unit:** People who share income and expenses as an economic unit

The approach varies by jurisdiction. Form 122A-1 uses the number of people "in your household."

### For households larger than 4 persons

The median income table provides figures for 1, 2, 3, and 4 person households. For each additional person beyond 4, a fixed dollar amount is added (published with each update cycle, e.g., approximately $10,000-$11,000 per additional person depending on the state).

### Comparison Calculation (Form 122A-1, Lines 12-14)

```
Line 12a: Total CMI (from Line 11)
Line 12b: CMI x 12 = Annualized income
Line 13:  Applicable state median family income for household size
Line 14:  Compare Line 12b to Line 13
  - If 12b <= 13: No presumption of abuse (PASS - check box 1)
  - If 12b > 13:  Must complete Form 122A-2 (check box 2)
```

### Data Source URLs

- Current median income tables: https://www.justice.gov/ust/means-testing
- Tables are published with effective dates (e.g., cases filed on or after November 1, 2025; April 1, 2026; etc.)

#### Total Deductions (Line 38)

```
Line 38 = Line 24 (IRS allowances)
        + Line 32 (additional deductions)
        + Line 37 (debt payment deductions)
```

### Part 3: Determine Whether There Is a Presumption of Abuse (Lines 39-42)

#### Step 1: Calculate 60-Month Disposable Income (Line 39)

```
Line 39a: Adjusted current monthly income (from Line 4)
Line 39b: Total deductions (from Line 38)
Line 39c: Monthly disposable income = 39a - 39b
Line 39d: 60-month total = 39c x 60
```

#### Step 2: Apply Thresholds (Line 40)

| Condition | Result |
|-----------|--------|
| Line 39d < $9,075 | **No presumption of abuse** (PASS) -- go to Part 5 |
| Line 39d > $15,150 | **Presumption of abuse** (FAIL) -- may claim special circumstances in Part 4 |
| $9,075 <= Line 39d <= $15,150 | Go to Line 41 for the 25% test |

> **Note:** The $9,075 and $15,150 thresholds are subject to adjustment every 3 years. Current values apply to cases filed April 1, 2025 through March 31, 2028. (Original values were $7,025/$11,725, adjusted to $8,175/$13,650 on April 1, 2022, then to $9,075/$15,150 on April 1, 2025.)

#### Step 3: The 25% Test (Lines 41-42)

For debtors in the middle range ($9,075-$15,150):

```
Line 41a: Total nonpriority unsecured debt (from Schedule or Official Form 106Sum, Line 3b)
Line 41b: 25% of nonpriority unsecured debt = Line 41a x 0.25
Line 42:  Compare Line 39d to Line 41b
  - If 39d < 41b:  No presumption of abuse (PASS)
  - If 39d >= 41b: Presumption of abuse (FAIL)
```

**In plain terms:** Even if 60-month disposable income is between the thresholds, there is no presumption of abuse if that amount is insufficient to pay 25% of the debtor's nonpriority unsecured debt.

### Part 4: Special Circumstances (Line 43)

Debtors facing a presumption of abuse may claim **special circumstances** that justify additional expenses or income adjustments for which there is no reasonable alternative. 11 U.S.C. Section 707(b)(2)(B).

Requirements:
- Itemize each additional expense or income adjustment
- Provide a detailed written explanation of the special circumstances
- Provide documentation of actual expenses or income adjustments to the case trustee
- Attest under oath to accuracy

Examples given in the statute: serious medical condition, call to active military duty.

### Part 5: Signature

Declarations under penalty of perjury for Debtor 1 and Debtor 2.

## 9. Presumption of Abuse: Trigger and Rebuttal

### What Triggers the Presumption

The presumption of abuse is triggered when:

1. **60-month disposable income exceeds $15,150**, OR
2. **60-month disposable income is between $9,075 and $15,150** AND that amount is sufficient to pay at least **25% of the debtor's nonpriority unsecured debt**

### How to Rebut the Presumption

Per 11 U.S.C. Section 707(b)(2)(B), the presumption may **only** be rebutted by demonstrating **special circumstances** such as:
- A serious medical condition
- A call or order to active duty in the Armed Forces
- Other circumstances that justify additional expenses or income adjustments **for which there is no reasonable alternative**

**Requirements for rebuttal:**
1. Itemize each additional expense or income adjustment
2. Provide detailed explanation of why the special circumstances make the expenses necessary and reasonable
3. Provide documentation of actual expenses or income adjustments to the case trustee
4. Attest under oath to accuracy of information

**Mathematical effect:** The special circumstances must cause the recalculated 60-month disposable income to fall below the applicable threshold (either $9,075 or the 25% test).

### Totality of Circumstances (Section 707(b)(3))

Even if the means test does not create a presumption of abuse, the court may still dismiss a Chapter 7 case under Section 707(b)(3) based on the **totality of the circumstances** -- considering bad faith or the overall financial picture.

## 10. Relationship to Schedule I

### Schedule I: Current Income of Individual Debtor(s)

Schedule I (Official Form 106I) reports the debtor's **current** income at the time of filing. It differs from the means test in several key ways:

| Aspect | Form 122A-1 (Means Test) | Schedule I |
|--------|--------------------------|------------|
| **Time period** | Average of prior 6 months | Current monthly income at filing |
| **Purpose** | Statutory means test calculation | Court's assessment of actual financial situation |
| **Income basis** | Gross income from all sources | Detailed employment income plus other sources |
| **Spouse treatment** | Column B for non-filing spouse in same household | Separate section for spouse's income |
| **Social Security** | Excluded from CMI calculation | Included (reported for full picture) |
| **Deductions** | Not shown (raw gross figures) | Shows payroll deductions to arrive at take-home |

### How Data Maps Between Forms

| Means Test Input | Schedule I Equivalent |
|------------------|-----------------------|
| Line 2: Gross wages/salary | Schedule I Lines 1-8 (employment income, but Schedule I shows more detail including employer info, pay frequency) |
| Line 3: Alimony | Schedule I Line 11 or similar |
| Line 5: Business income | Schedule I Line 8a-8b (self-employment) |
| Line 8: Unemployment | Schedule I other income lines |
| Line 9: Pension/retirement | Schedule I Line 10 or other income |

**Key difference:** The means test uses a **backward-looking 6-month average** while Schedule I reflects the debtor's **current** situation. A debtor who recently lost a job might have high CMI (from the lookback period) but low Schedule I income. Both forms are filed, and the court considers both.

## 12. Implementation Notes for Software Developers

### Calculation Flow

1. **Collect debtor demographics:** state, county, household size, ages, marital/filing status
2. **Collect 6 months of income data** for debtor and (if applicable) non-filing spouse
3. **Calculate CMI** (sum all included income for 6 months, divide by 6)
4. **Look up applicable median income** for state + household size from U.S. Trustee tables
5. **Compare annualized CMI to median:** if at or below, STOP -- no presumption of abuse
6. **If above median:** calculate adjusted CMI (subtract non-household spouse income)
7. **Look up all applicable IRS standards** (National for food/clothing/health; Local for housing by county, transportation by region/MSA)
8. **Calculate each deduction line** (standards-based and actual expenses)
9. **Calculate secured debt payments** (total contractual due over 60 months / 60 for each)
10. **Calculate priority debt payments** (total past-due / 60)
11. **Sum all deductions**
12. **Calculate monthly disposable income** (adjusted CMI - total deductions)
13. **Multiply by 60** for the 60-month figure
14. **Apply thresholds:** <$9,075 = pass; >$15,150 = fail; between = apply 25% test
15. **If between thresholds:** compare to 25% of nonpriority unsecured debt

### Edge Cases and Gotchas

1. **Lookback period timing:** The 6-month period ends on the last day of the month BEFORE filing. Filing date strategy can materially affect CMI.
2. **Seasonal or variable income:** Must average all 6 months even if income varied significantly.
3. **Household size ambiguity:** Different courts use different definitions. Software should allow manual override.
4. **Line 5 vs. household size:** The "number of people" for deduction purposes (Line 5 of 122A-2) may differ from household size used for median income comparison (Line 13 of 122A-1). Line 5 uses tax exemptions + additional supported dependents.
5. **Secured debt double-counting prevention:** Vehicle and home payments appear in both the Local Standards section (Lines 9b, 13b, 13e) and the debt payment section (Lines 33a-c). The net standard amounts (Lines 9c, 13c, 13f) account for this by subtracting actual payments from the standard. The actual payments then appear again in Line 33 as debt deductions. This is intentional -- the debtor gets the greater of the standard or actual payment.
6. **Minimum $0 rule:** Net amounts on Lines 9c, 13c, 13f cannot go below $0.
7. **Vehicle ownership only with payments:** The ownership/lease standard (Lines 13a, 13d) can only be claimed if the debtor actually makes loan or lease payments on the vehicle.
8. **Social Security carve-out:** Must be tracked but excluded from CMI. Unemployment under the Social Security Act is also excluded but must be reported separately.
9. **Applicable date for standards:** The IRS standards and median income figures applicable to a case are determined by the filing date, not the date of form preparation.
10. **Non-filing spouse income:** Included in CMI calculation but can be partially backed out on 122A-2 Line 3 for income not used for debtor's household.
